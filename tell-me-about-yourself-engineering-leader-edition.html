Question 1 of 5
"When was the last time you promoted someone on your team? How did it happen?"
A few years ago, a recent college grad on my team earned 4 AWS certifications in a single calendar year — while pursuing a master's degree. My job was to create the conditions: feedback on communication across every channel, visibility with stakeholders outside our team, and a year-end case that wrote itself.

He became the first person I promoted to senior engineer. Today he serves as tech lead of his own team. A second engineer — who led more by example than words — made principal after I shifted my advocacy for her: pushing her written contributions and technical writing to the foreground.
🏆
Your job is to build the case — even when your org approves promotions above your level.

02
Question 2 of 5
"How do you nurture psychological safety in your team?"
I joined a new team as manager after WFH due to COVID-19 began — Zoom was the only avenue to build rapport. Weekly 1:1s were table stakes. But the move that shifted the culture was starting a technical book club.

The goal was exposure to design principles beyond our daily scope. The side effect: engineers who seldom spoke beyond daily stand-ups were now leading discussions — and their ideas started showing up in production code.

Psychological safety isn't announced. It's built through repeated, low-stakes opportunities to be heard.
📖
Create rituals that let quieter people lead. Then watch what they build.
03
Question 3 of 5
"When did you last support a direct report's growth — even if it meant losing them?"
A struggling contractor team needed a leader — not more code. I proposed it as a stretch opportunity for my best engineer. Senior leadership bought in.

He thrived. Got promoted. Joined Amazon as an engineering team lead. Moved to principal software engineering manager at Microsoft. He recently returned to our organization as a senior manager.

That outcome didn't hurt my team. It built my reputation as a leader worth working for — the kind that accelerates careers instead of hoarding talent.
🚀
The leaders people remember most are the ones who made them better — even at their own short-term cost.
04
Question 4 of 5
"Can I speak to women on your team about their experience?"
Two of the engineers on my previous team are women. I helped both build track records which resulted in promotions to principal engineer.

One of the two has since been promoted to tech lead and moved to another team with broader scope. That's a win, not a loss.

A leader who is afraid of this question is telling you something about how they view their people's potential. The right answer isn't just "yes" — it's having done the work to make "yes" safe to say.
🎯
Your team's experience of you should be the same whether they're being observed or not.
05
Question 5 of 5
"Why did the last person in this role leave?"
This is the one question most interviewers forget to ask — and the most revealing one a candidate can ask a hiring manager.

A leader who can't answer it hasn't been paying attention. A leader who answers it honestly — including what they'd do differently — is showing you exactly how they handle accountability.

The question works both ways: if you're the hiring manager being asked, your answer reveals how self-aware, honest, and coachable you actually are.
🔍
The best candidates will ask this. Have a real answer — and use it to show what you've learned.
The Framework
Judge your next hiring manager by these 5 questions.
Their answers will tell you more than their job description ever will.
1
Promotions: Do they build cases proactively — or wait for their reports to self-advocate?
2
Safety: Can they name specific rituals that build trust, not just state values?
3
Growth: Have they ever sacrificed short-term team stability for a direct report's long-term career?
4
Equity: Would underrepresented members of their team say they're treated as fairly as everyone else?
5
Accountability: Can they explain clearly and honestly why their last report left?
